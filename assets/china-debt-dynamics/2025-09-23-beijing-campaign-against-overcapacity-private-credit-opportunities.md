# Beijing’s Campaign Against Overcapacity Creates Private Credit Opportunities

**Series:** China Debt Dynamics  
**Volume / Issue:** Volume 9, Issue 4  
**Date:** September 23, 2025

## Executive Summary

ShoreVest’s private credit strategy is built on three interrelated approaches:

1. Asset-backed lending  
2. Asset restructuring  
3. Debt resolutions and distressed debt investing (including non-performing loans, or NPLs)

Periods of deleveraging, economic transition, and stabilization tend to generate significant private credit opportunities. In China’s current transition from legacy growth drivers to more advanced sectors, overcapacity has become a key source of such opportunities.

---

## Why Overcapacity Matters Now

Overcapacity is one of China’s most pressing macroeconomic challenges. It has contributed to:

- Entrenched producer price deflation
- Weaker corporate profitability
- Declining government tax revenues

In response, Beijing has elevated overcapacity reduction to a top policy priority. The campaign remains in early stages, with unresolved questions around:

- Who should absorb closure costs
- How quickly excess capacity should be removed
- How distressed or underutilized assets should be repurposed

This is likely the beginning of a multi-year transition requiring creative financing and restructuring expertise.

---

## Sunset vs. Sunrise Overcapacity

Overcapacity in China can be grouped into two broad categories.

### 1) Sunset Industries

These are sectors tied to the former housing-led growth model, including:

- Steel
- Flat glass
- Cement
- Home appliances
- Construction machinery

Demand in these sectors is unlikely to return to pre-2021 peak levels. Many firms have shifted toward export markets, often through aggressive price cuts, increasing external trade tensions.

### 2) Sunrise Industries

These are strategic sectors Beijing aims to promote, including:

- Electric vehicles
- Batteries
- Solar panels

Overcapacity in sunrise sectors has been driven by:

- Local governments broadly supporting expansion and prolonging weak firms
- Corporate strategies focused on market share over near-term profit

Because local support has delayed market exits, industrial consolidation has moved more slowly than expected.

---

## Policy Shift: From Signaling to Enforcement

Beijing first identified overcapacity as a national challenge at the **Central Economic Work Conference in December 2023**. Early policy implementation was limited, and producer prices continued to decline through August (three consecutive years).

A stronger intervention phase began in late June and early July:

- **June 30:** *People’s Daily* criticized local governments for subsidy races, duplicative investment, and intervention that delayed capacity exits.
- **July 1:** *Qiushi* highlighted local protectionism and weaknesses in bankruptcy/restructuring implementation.
- **July 2:** The **Central Commission for Financial and Economic Affairs (CFFEA)** called for curbing destructive price wars and supporting orderly exit of outdated capacity.
- At the same CFFEA meeting, Xi Jinping emphasized accountability for local officials and correction of local protectionist behavior.

---

## Early Market Effects

By August, fixed asset investment declined in sectors associated with targeted overcapacity reduction:

- Metal smelting and pressing: **-16.7% YoY**
- Electrical equipment manufacturing (including batteries and solar): **-9.5%**
- Chemical processing/manufacturing: **-8.7%**

While investment slowdowns may prevent further buildup, decommissioning existing facilities remains the harder challenge.

---

## Core Constraint: Who Pays for Capacity Exit?

The central practical issue is cost allocation. Potential bearers include:

- Local governments
- Equity holders
- Creditors
- Workers
- Hybrid burden-sharing structures

### Polysilicon as a Live Case Study

China’s polysilicon sector (solar feedstock) is one of the most advanced examples of coordinated capacity reduction:

- Leading firms have discussed creating a **RMB 50 billion** fund
- Fund purpose: acquire outdated smaller plants and shut them
- Target capacity reduction: from roughly **3.0–3.2 million metric tons** to around **2.0 million tons** annually
- Estimated 2025 demand: **1.4–1.6 million tons**

Even this reduced capacity would remain above demand, but could help stabilize pricing.

Negotiations have proven complex, especially around contribution formulas and asset pricing. A Q3 target for agreement has likely slipped into 2026.

---

## Timeline and Macroeconomic Trade-Off

Beijing appears to be balancing urgency against systemic risk:

- **Too slow:** deflation persists, domestic demand weakens
- **Too fast:** layoffs, bankruptcies, and fiscal stress for local governments could intensify

Rongsheng Petrochemical has indicated expectations that around **10%** of petrochemical installed capacity may be cut over **three to five years**, suggesting a gradual adjustment path. This is broadly consistent with the 2015–2019 overcapacity campaign duration.

---

## Private Credit Implications

A broad overcapacity campaign is likely to expand demand for:

- M&A-led consolidation
- Corporate restructuring
- Distressed asset disposal and collateral repurposing

Banks will participate, but many transactions require specialized credit structuring and operational restructuring capabilities—areas where private credit investors can play a differentiated role.

What began as overcapacity concerns concentrated in cleantech may now widen into a larger economy-wide restructuring cycle.
