The LP stress test for China private credit managers

Serious allocators do not underwrite a strategy from positioning statements. They underwrite the evidence chain behind each claim.

This LP stress test is designed for investment committees, consultants, and ODD teams assessing specialist China private credit platforms.

What institutional LPs will probe first

  • Track-record basis, scope, and exclusions — especially any “no principal loss” statement.
  • Continuity between predecessor platforms and the current manager entity.
  • How collateral-first underwriting is implemented in downside-case memos.
  • What PRC enforceability means in documentation, perfection, ranking, and real execution timelines.
  • How deal-level outcomes translate into fund-level net returns after drag, costs, and timing.

What weak answers sound like

Generic phrases such as “deep relationships,” “strong local team,” or “disciplined underwriting” do not survive institutional diligence.

LPs require definitions, boundaries, and evidence of repeatability — not slogans.

What credible answers must include

  • Clear separation of team-level, deal-level, and fund-level records.
  • Precise definitions for principal protection, write-downs, restructurings, and near-loss outcomes.
  • Named accountability across sourcing, underwriting, legal execution, servicing, and resolution.
  • Concentration disclosures by borrower, collateral type, province/city, court system, and sector.
  • Empirical support for correlation claims and crisis-period behavior.
  • Operational detail on internal versus outsourced servicing and conflict management.

The core allocator question

The most important diligence test is whether recoverability is driven by legal position, asset control, and execution — rather than optimistic market rebound assumptions.

If a manager can evidence that chain in a representative transaction from sourcing to recovery, the broader platform narrative becomes credible.

Representative LP diligence agenda (18 modules)

  • Track-record credibility and treatment of weakest realized outcomes.
  • 2004-to-present continuity and what changed when the current platform was established.
  • Collateral valuation, haircut framework, and decline discipline.
  • PRC enforceability workflow and formal court versus negotiated resolution usage.
  • True source of edge: sourcing, legal position, execution, or complexity pricing.
  • Pipeline quality: proprietary vs intermediated vs auction and conversion rates.
  • In-house servicing depth and case evidence where internal control improved outcomes.
  • Concentration and portfolio construction constraints.
  • Risk appetite implied by principal-protection posture.
  • Fund-level economics after fees, timing drag, legal costs, and currency realities.
  • Correlation claims, driver decomposition, and stress-period behavior.
  • Team accountability map, key-person concentration, and succession resilience.
  • China policy and regulatory scenarios that could impair recoveries.
  • Why now: cyclical entry opportunity versus enduring platform edge.
  • Portfolio-fit case against broad Asia special situations or global distressed sleeves.
  • Investor reporting depth, cadence, valuation approach, and exception handling.
  • Institutional infrastructure test: platform reality versus website narrative.
  • End-to-end transaction walkthrough with explicit failure points.

Bottom line

A specialist manager can pass rigorous LP scrutiny when its strongest claims are defined, bounded, and consistently evidenced.

The diligence burden is not to “win the argument.” It is to prove that recoverability is repeatable under legal and operational stress.
