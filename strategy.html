Market Context

Market dislocation is a structural entry condition.

China remains the world's second-largest credit market, yet many global portfolios remain materially under-allocated for non-fundamental reasons. That underexposure is itself an active risk decision.

ShoreVest's strategy focuses on where spread and collateral coverage improve when complexity reduces competition. Structure, legal priority, and execution cadence determine outcomes more than macro narrative.

Structural spreadComplexity can reduce competition and widen spread for enforceable secured claims.
Vintage effectPeriods of weaker sentiment have historically supported better entry basis and downside buffers.

Risk Framework

Risk controls and return profile

Downside controls are embedded in both deal underwriting and portfolio construction.

Underwriting controls

  • Conservative LTV/CTV bands with stressed recovery assumptions.
  • Priority claims and enforceability review under PRC-law documentation.
  • No reliance on refinancing to realize value.

Portfolio controls

  • Diversification by borrower, collateral, province, and vintage.
  • Continuous surveillance from origination through disposition.
  • Predefined exit pathways to reduce duration and execution risk.
Target Profile

Risk-adjusted return profile

The strategy targets approximately 10–15% net returns in USD terms without fund-level leverage, emphasizing current cash yield, principal protection, and shorter realization duration.

Portfolio construction implication

China onshore secured credit is driven primarily by collateral recovery, legal process, and execution quality rather than public spread or equity beta factors.

For portfolios concentrated in public credit and equities, the strategy has historically shown lower correlation to broad benchmarks. Low correlation does not eliminate risk or protect against loss.
